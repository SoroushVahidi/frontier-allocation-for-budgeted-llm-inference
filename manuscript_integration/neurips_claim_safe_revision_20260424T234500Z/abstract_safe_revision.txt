We study adaptive reasoning-budget allocation as an operational controller for bounded-action inference and evaluate it under an explicit action-budget contract. Rather than treating the controller score as a single closed-form objective, we provide an implementation-level specification of branch state, answer-group aggregation, family-aware anti-collapse controls, commit thresholds, and bounded output repair. This formulation enables reproducible diagnostics about when adaptive allocation helps and where it remains fragile.

On the matched manuscript evaluation surface, frontier-style configurations are competitive and provide informative gains over selected internal variants, while comparisons between Strict-F3 and Strict-Gate1-Cap-K6 are supportive but not uniformly decisive across slices. Comparisons to near-direct external baselines are mixed, so we frame results as bounded competitiveness rather than universal dominance. We additionally report held-out and real-model audits as robustness-oriented appendix evidence that helps define claim boundaries, including surface sensitivity and accounting caveats.

Overall, the contribution is a claim-safe empirical and methodological package: an operationally specified controller, transparent statistical diagnostics, and conservative interpretation rules for action-budget-matched reasoning experiments.
