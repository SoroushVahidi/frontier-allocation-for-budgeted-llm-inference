We study adaptive reasoning-budget allocation as an operational controller for bounded-action inference and evaluate it under an explicit action-budget contract. Rather than treating the controller score as a single closed-form objective, we provide an implementation-level specification of branch state, answer-group aggregation, family-aware anti-collapse controls, commit thresholds, and bounded output repair. A targeted anti-collapse calibration sweep on the matched manuscript surface shows the default anti-collapse setting is overactive (weak anti-collapse performs best, off also beats default, and conditional underperforms), so anti-collapse is framed as a calibration-sensitive design axis rather than a universally validated independent gain. This formulation enables reproducible diagnostics about when adaptive allocation helps and where it remains fragile.

On the matched manuscript evaluation surface, the leading frontier-allocation variants form a statistically close top internal cluster. In particular, Strict-F3 and Strict-Gate1-Cap-K6 are statistically close on paired/bootstrap/permutation slices, so the exact internal winner is surface-dependent rather than decisive. We use Strict-F3 as a representative for continuity and diagnostics, not as a universal winner claim. Comparisons to near-direct external baselines are slice-specific under matched action-budget/substrate conditions, so we frame these as bounded competitiveness rather than across-all-slice dominance. Held-out and real-model audits are reported as appendix robustness evidence for claim-boundary calibration.

Overall, the contribution is a claim-safe empirical and methodological package: an operationally specified frontier-allocation formulation, diagnostic evidence linking budget use to branch-level allocation and absent-from-tree bottlenecks, and conservative interpretation rules for action-budget-matched reasoning experiments.
